Introduction

With the increasing number user of Front’s iOS app, we decided to rethink the composing experience. For this project, we focused on context and speed around replying to conversations.
User were not able to get the full context of a conversation while replying to a message, increasing the reply time and lowering the quality of the replies.

Replying with context

Replying to a message can be strait-forward, but sometime it requires more effort.
That’s why having access to the full history of a conversation can be helpful in a lot of situations. In Front, not only you can be looking for information in past messages, but also from activities and comments added by your team previously. That’s why it became clear to us that the user should be able to compose and browse a conversation at the same time.

A flexible experience

In addition to our main goal, we took this opportunity to also simplify and enhance the composing experience. By removing advanced features and focusing on the core experience, we were able to provide the right amount of flexibility at the right time. In order to build this new experience we focused on a multi states composer. Instead of having a single fullscreen experience for the composer, we decided to split it into 3 different states:
- Collapsed: The content of the reply is not visible. This mode is used when the composing experience is paused and the content of the conversation is main element of focus.
- Adaptive: The composer will be responsive, based on the content of the reply. This allows the user to focus both on the content of the conversation and the composition of the reply.
- Expanded: The composer is shown above the rest of conversation, above its content. It can be used for longer messages.

Multi channels

While a lot of conversations the app are emails, Front supports a long list of different channel types. Messenger, Twitter, Twilio and others are available. This implies that the composer needs to support additional features and provide an optimized experience for those different type of channels. The Twitter composer is providing user autocomplete as well as a character counter. The Twilio composer is displaying a SMS counter.

Other explorations

Some explorations focused on a mini toolbar, used to provide more option to the composer. The initial exploration focus only on replying with text, and we wanted to incorporate some attachment management.
We ended up revisiting the signature UI and switched from a floating pill to a new action available in the mini toolbar.
We also explored an attachment viewer with the ability to see file name, file sizes and image previews.

Outcome

After the release of this new composer, we measured the adoption of the new composer. 75% of our users rely on the new quick reply mode while 25% prefer to use the fullscreen mode.
This project was also the opportunity for the design team to focus on providing more adaptive experiences in the app. By allowing the user to make the UI adapts to their need, while offering more context for a better productivity.
